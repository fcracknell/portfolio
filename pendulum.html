Swing-Up and LQR Control Simulation of an Inverted Pendulum

Introduction and Project Overview

Control is a fundamental element of aerospace engineering and plays a major role in providing a solution to a broad range of real-world challenges such as flight dynamics and spacecraft attitude control. This project uses the classic inverted pendulum, a well-documented and fundamental control problem, as a springboard from which to dive in and learn more about what it takes to tackle these real world control problems.

Using a custom Python physics simulation and MATLAB-based controller design, this project saw the successful implementation of an energy-based swing-up strategy, proportional-integral-derivative (PID) control, and ultimately a full-state linear quadratic regulator (LQR) controller to stabilise both the pendulum angle and cart position simultaneously.

Background Theory

The inverted pendulum system consists of a cart with a given mass, constrained to move in one dimension along the horizontal direction, and a pendulum with some other mass connected to this cart by (in this case) a massless rod. The desired state of this system is for the pendulum to be vertically upright with no angular velocity, and the cart to also be stationary at some desired horizontal position. The challenge of the inverted pendulum problem arises when we restrict ourselves to only being able to control the system by applying a force to the cart.

Ordinarily however, think about balancing a tall broomstick using only the palm of your hand and with the brush in the air. When the system is inverted, the inherent instability of this state makes it incredibly difficult for it to be maintained. For this reason, it makes sense to begin with a pendulum in a downwards position, as this is the realistic natural state for a pendulum you may come across in real life.

Therefore, it is necessary to find some way of swinging up the pendulum in a precise enough manner that allows it to approach this desired state. In fact, it turns out that using some basic energy conservation principles and a bit of maths, not only is it possible to precisely swing-up the pendulum, but it is also possible to control the maintain the desired upright position of the pendulum by merely applying some variable force to the cart.

By drawing an analogy with a roller coaster, we can think of the energy of the pendulum as being broken down into two parts: gravitational potential energy, and kinetic energy. We therefore define the desired energy to be equal to the energy of the stationary pendulum in its inverted state, purely gravitational potential energy. Returning to the rollercoaster, if at some point on its way up an incline, the sum of its gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy is greater than orequal to the gravitational potential energy at the top of the incline, then we know that it will make it up the hill. Applying this to the pendulum, we apply a force to the cart in a direction that accelerates the pendulum, therefore increasing its kinetic energy. Once the pendulum has been swung-up, the challenge shifts to actively maintaining the inherently unstable upright position of the now inverted pendulum. This is where control methods become essential.

To Stabilise the pendulum, we implement a Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR), a full-state feedback control strategy that allows both the pendulum angle and cart position to be controlled. Alternatively, a more simple proportional-integral-derivative (PID) controller could be implemented, but its authority is limited to just the pendulum angle, meaning the cart continues moving on into infinity!

Without getting too far into the details, an LQR controller computes a way of applying force to the cart purely based on the current state of the system (cart position, cart velocity, pendulum angle, and pendulum angular velocity). It achieves this by modelling the dynamics of the cart-pendulum system as a set of linearised state-space equations about the upright equilibrium position. The applied force (system input) is calculated by each state variable (cart position, cart velocity, pendulum angle, and pendulum angular velocity) by a given constant (gain) and summing the result. The state variables (system output) are then observed once more and a new input force is calculated, forming a closed-loop control algorithm that stabilises when the pendulum is stationary and upright, and the cart is stationary.
